Today we put on our good jeans, comb our hair, and throw open the doors to introduce Fathoms to the neighborhood. Welcome, new friends! Please, take your shoes off at the door.

Education reform is complex. Our aim is really quite simple: to make information about your California public schools easy to find and understand. Do with it what you will.

Right now, Fathoms has a profile page for nearly every public high school in the state. On each you'll find:

  1. the schools score on the statewide Academic Performance Index
  2. the percentage of graduates that pass the California High School Exit Exams in Math and English
  3. the percentage of 11th and 12th grade students that take an AP exam, and the score breakdown
  4. the average school SAT score by section
  5. as well as basic school contact information

But we're looking to add more. More data. More schools. And better explanations as to what it all means.

(Check out the walk-through of each section for more details.)

We understand that standardized testing can be a hot button issue. We've seen documentaries, read articles or books, and heard first hand accounts of the flaws of standardized tests. These issues are very real, and they exist in how they're created, how they are given, and how we interpret the results.

This leads some to see it as a costly waste of students' time, and reason to scale back or eliminate standardized testing altogether. Others take the perspective that no test will be perfect, and that what we learn from standardized testing make them worthwhile.

Personally, I find value in standardized testing scores. I find value in knowing how students in different schools are doing on the tests. If only because it sets up the more important question of "Why are students at different schools performing differently on the tests?"

But that's just me, and I'm an expert in exactly nothing (except, perhaps, mid-2000s indie rock).

And I acknowledge that intelligent and well-intentioned people hold opinions that span the spectrum.

But regardless of opinion, as long as students are spending classroom time taking statewide tests, and as long as colleges are looking at SAT scores in the admission process, and college credit is being awarding for passed AP tests, then standardized testing is a crucial element of our education system.

As such, parents, teachers & students should not have search all over for this information. It should be easy to find and understand. And that's really our aim.
